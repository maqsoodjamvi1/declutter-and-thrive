When I look at my guest room, I don’t see a welcoming sanctuary. I see the place where “temporary” boxes go to die. Over the last two years, my guest room became a landing strip for holiday decorations, off-season coats, and that treadmill I swore I’d use daily. If your spare room has become a storage unit, you aren’t alone. Most of us treat the guest room like a junk drawer with a door.

Getting it ready for visitors requires more than just changing the sheets. It requires a strategy to move long-term storage out of the space without just shoving it into a closet that will burst open the moment a guest hangs up a jacket.

The 24-Hour Rule for Sorting

The biggest mistake people make is trying to organize the entire room in one weekend. Instead, I use a “24-hour staging” approach. Take everything currently living on the floor or bed and move it to the center of the room. Don’t try to sort it where it stands.

Once it’s in the middle, create three physical zones using painter’s tape on the floor: Keep, Donate, and Rehome. The “Rehome” zone is critical. This is for items that don’t belong in a guest room—like the box of tax documents that belongs in your home office, or the kitchen appliances that should be in the pantry. If you spend more than 30 seconds deciding where an item goes, it’s a sign you’re overthinking. Throw it in the “Rehome” pile and move to the next item.

Dealing with the “Guest Closet” Paradox

If your guest room closet is currently filled with your own clothes, your guests will end up living out of their suitcases on the floor. That creates a claustrophobic feeling.

To clear the closet effectively:

  • Remove the seasonal swap: If it’s currently summer, take all your heavy winter sweaters and boots out of the guest closet. Store them under your own bed or in a high-up shelf in your master bedroom.
  • Install “command hooks” on the back of the door: Instead of using the floor for luggage racks or shoe piles, use the vertical space on the inside of the closet door for robes or light jackets.
  • Clear the bottom 18 inches: This is the golden zone for suitcases. If you can clear the floor of the closet completely, your guest has a designated, out-of-the-way spot to open their bag, which prevents the room from feeling like a hotel locker room.

The Bed-as-Storage Trap

Many of us use the space under the bed for long-term storage. If you have guests coming, check what is actually under there. If it’s bins of old paperwork or sentimental items that rarely get touched, that’s fine. But if it’s things you might need during their stay—like spare linens or towels—you need to move those items to an accessible shelf.

If you have a bed skirt, make sure it’s actually hiding the bins. If the bins are visible, it looks messy regardless of how clean the room is. A quick tip: if you don’t have room to move those bins elsewhere, cover them with a neutral-colored fabric that matches the bedspread. It turns a “clutter storage” look into a “designed base” look.

Handling the “Just in Case” Mentality

We keep things in the guest room because we fear we’ll need them “someday.” When you’re clearing the room, you will find items like old hobby gear, half-finished projects, or redundant kitchen gadgets.

To break this cycle, ask yourself one question: “If I were a guest, would I feel comfortable sleeping next to this item?” If the answer is no, it doesn’t belong in the room. Sentimental clutter is the hardest to move, but remember that the goal is to make the room functional for someone else. Move the sentimental items to a dedicated storage bin in the garage or basement, and clear the room’s horizontal surfaces (nightstands, dressers) entirely.

The Finishing Touches for a Guest-Ready Space

Once the floor is clear and the closet is functional, you need to verify the “landing zone.” A guest room should have at least one empty flat surface. If your guest room desk or dresser is covered in your personal mail or knick-knacks, clear it off.

I suggest keeping a small tray on the dresser. It gives guests a place to put their keys, watch, or jewelry so they don’t lose those items in your house. When you clear the room, you are essentially telling your guest that their comfort is more important than your storage needs. That one mindset shift makes the whole process feel less like a chore and more like an act of hospitality.

Now that you have a plan to reclaim your space and make it truly inviting, it is time to consider the small comfort items that turn a tidy room into a guest retreat. Beyond just clearing out the clutter, you might want to look into organizational tools that keep the room clean long after your visitors have checked out.
